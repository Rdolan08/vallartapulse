Refine the VallartaPulse pricing tool after the larger Greater Puerto Vallarta scrape is complete.

We are now moving beyond a small Zona Romántica and Amapas sample. Rework the pricing engine so it behaves like a true Puerto Vallarta short-term rental pricing tool and not a generic STR calculator.

Primary objective

Rebuild the pricing model so it reflects what actually drives nightly rates in Puerto Vallarta and surrounding markets:

1. Seasonality is one of the most important pricing drivers and must be treated as a major input
2. Building and unit quality matter more than simple beach distance
3. Micro-location matters more than broad neighborhood labels
4. Rooftop pool and premium common areas matter a lot
5. The model must support a much larger Greater PV geography
6. Outputs must be explainable and believable to owners and property managers

Critical market-specific requirement: seasonality

In Puerto Vallarta, seasonality is absolutely critical and must be weighted heavily in the model.

General seasonal pattern:
- High season generally begins in October
- High season runs through May
- Summer is rainy season and is materially weaker for pricing and demand
- June through September should generally be treated as lower-demand season unless a specific demand driver is present

Seasonality cannot be a minor modifier. It should be a core pricing layer.

The pricing engine must support:
- monthly seasonality factors
- holiday and event overlays
- shoulder season behavior
- low season / rainy season discounts
- premium peak periods

At minimum, create a seasonal demand calendar for Puerto Vallarta with adjustable multipliers.

Use these broad market assumptions as a starting point:
- October: start of recovery into high season
- November: strong high season, including US and Canadian Thanksgiving travel
- December: high season with major peak around Christmas and New Year’s
- January: very strong high season, including MLK weekend
- February: very strong high season, including Bear Week / Beef Dip and golf-related demand
- March: very strong high season, including Spring Break demand
- April: strong high season, often influenced by Easter / Semana Santa
- May: still strong, especially around Pride, but beginning to soften later in the month
- June: meaningful seasonal drop
- July: low / rainy season
- August: low / rainy season
- September: typically weakest or among the weakest months
- October again ramps up into high season

Build event overlays for the following demand peaks where applicable:
- Christmas
- New Year’s
- Bear Week / Beef Dip
- major golf tournament periods
- Pride
- Easter / Semana Santa
- Spring Break
- MLK weekend
- US Thanksgiving
- Canadian Thanksgiving

Architect this so event and holiday premiums can stack on top of baseline monthly seasonality.

Important: rainy season matters
Summertime in PV is rainy season and that should be explicitly reflected in the pricing model. This is not a subtle effect. The engine should recognize that summer demand is generally weaker and should price accordingly unless a listing has unusual strengths or a special event period offsets the softness.

Location logic

Distance to beach should remain in the model, but it should not dominate. In PV, many units are relatively close to the beach compared with other markets, so the model should focus more on quality and micro-location nuance.

Add location features such as:
- nearest beach access distance
- walkability to restaurants, nightlife, beach clubs, and the Malecón
- slope or hillside penalty where relevant
- cross streets
- ocean view / partial ocean view / city view / no view
- direct beach access or not
- “quiet luxury” areas versus high-energy walkable areas
- proximity to top landmarks and premium corridors

Cross streets and building names should become important pricing clues.

Building-level intelligence

This is a major improvement area.

For each recognized building:
- compute building median nightly rate
- compute building rate by bedroom bucket
- compute building sample size
- compute building quality tier
- compute building seasonal behavior if enough data exists
- flag thin sample buildings

Create a building premium layer that can strongly influence pricing.

If the selected unit is in a recognized building with enough sample size, anchor heavily to building-level comps before falling back to neighborhood-level logic.

Building-level drivers may include:
- newer construction
- strong reputation
- design quality
- rooftop pool
- direct beach access or beach club access
- gym
- concierge or staffed lobby
- luxury common areas
- stronger views
- consistent unit quality within the building

Amenities UI cleanup

The amenities selector is too long and clunky.

Replace it with a tighter grouped UI using chips, toggles, or dropdowns.

Suggested amenity groups:
- Views: ocean, partial ocean, city, garden, none
- Building amenities: rooftop pool, regular pool, gym, elevator, concierge, parking
- Lifestyle amenities: beach access, pet friendly, workspace, washer/dryer
- Luxury amenities: hot tub, terrace, luxury kitchen, designer interior

Weight major amenities more heavily than small convenience amenities.
Rooftop pool should have substantially more pricing power than a standard pool.
Building amenities should generally matter more than minor in-unit amenities.

Pricing model architecture

Refactor the engine into layers in this order:

Layer 1: Seasonal market base
Calculate a base nightly rate using:
- month / seasonal period
- event overlays if applicable
- broad market area
- submarket
- bedroom bucket
- bathroom bucket

Layer 2: Building anchor
If the building is known and has enough sample size:
- anchor strongly to building median / building comps
- apply building-specific seasonal behavior if available

Layer 3: Micro-location adjustment
Adjust for:
- cross streets
- view class
- beach access
- walkability
- hillside penalty
- premium corridor bonus

Layer 4: Unit quality adjustment
Adjust for the quality score.

Layer 5: Amenity adjustment
Apply premiums for major amenities, especially rooftop pool and premium common areas.

Layer 6: Confidence adjustment
Incorporate data quality, comp quality, building certainty, and sample depth.

Layer 7: Guardrails
Prevent unrealistic outputs with:
- min / max bounds by area and bedroom count
- outlier clipping
- thin-sample warnings
- confidence warnings

Comps engine improvements

Improve comp selection. Prioritize comps in this order:
1. same building
2. same micro-cluster or nearby cross streets
3. same submarket
4. same bedroom bucket
5. similar quality score
6. similar view class
7. similar amenity tier
8. similar seasonal period

De-prioritize listings that are geographically close but clearly lower quality or in a different building class.

Show comp explanations such as:
- 5 same-building comps used
- 8 nearby premium comps used
- 4 fallback submarket comps used
- seasonal weighting applied based on April high-season behavior

Confidence scoring

Every pricing output should include:
- recommended nightly rate
- low and high range
- confidence score
- explanation of main pricing drivers

Confidence should increase when:
- building is matched exactly
- many similar comps exist
- same season comps exist
- quality tier is clear
- area has strong coverage

Confidence should decrease when:
- building is unknown
- sample is thin
- listing type is unusual
- seasonality data is weak
- geography is underrepresented
